Pan-Hantavirus CD4+ Epitope Identification for Broad-Spectrum Vaccine Design

Using immunoinformatics approaches to identify conserved CD4+ epitopes for potential pan-hantavirus vaccine constructs.

Independent Research Vaccine Design Immunoinformatics 2021–2023

Project Motivation

Hantaviruses represent an emerging public health threat due to their high mortality rates and significant genomic diversity. Developing broadly protective vaccines is particularly challenging because of genetic variation across hantavirus species and the potential for viral evolution over time.

This project explored whether conserved CD4+ epitopes could be identified across known hantavirus genomes with the long-term goal of informing broad-spectrum or pan-hantavirus vaccine design.

My Role

This was an independent research project conducted under the mentorship of Dr. Melissa Willis at the Academies of Loudoun.

My work focused on computational epitope discovery, sequence analysis, immunoinformatics pipeline development, and biological interpretation of candidate vaccine targets.

Approach

An immunoinformatics workflow was developed to analyze 46 indexed hantavirus genomes obtained from NIH GenBank.

The workflow included:

  • Multiple sequence alignment of hantavirus genomes
  • Identification of conserved protein regions
  • Isolation of candidate 11+ mer epitopes
  • Prediction of MHC-II binding capacity
  • Antigenicity screening
  • Allergenicity prediction
  • Toxicity prediction

Candidate epitopes were filtered based on predicted immunogenicity, safety, and conservation across hantavirus species.

Results

The computational pipeline identified nine candidate conserved CD4+ epitopes that met filtering criteria for sequence length, predicted MHC-II binding, antigenicity, allergenicity, and toxicity.

Subsequent analysis revealed that the conserved regions associated with these epitopes were located primarily within highly hydrophobic transmembrane regions of the viral glycoprotein and membrane interface.

Although the project demonstrated that conserved regions could be computationally identified across hantavirus genomes, the biological accessibility and practical immunogenicity of these epitopes were likely limited in vivo.

Discussion & Limitations

One of the most important findings from this work was that sequence conservation alone does not necessarily imply vaccine viability. While conserved epitopes were identified computationally, their localization within hydrophobic transmembrane regions significantly reduced their practical immunogenic potential.

This highlighted an important challenge in computational vaccine design: biologically meaningful constraints must be considered alongside computational predictions.

The project also suggested that broad glycoprotein variability among hantavirus species may limit the feasibility of truly universal hantavirus vaccine targets.

Future Directions

Future work could focus on analyzing a more targeted set of clinically relevant hantavirus strains and evaluating whether functionally important viral motifs associated with infection could serve as more effective vaccine targets.

Additional investigation into the evolutionary dynamics and potential recombination patterns among hantavirus species may also help clarify the feasibility of pan-hantavirus vaccine development.

Reflection

This project was my first major introduction to scientific research and computational biology. Through this work, I learned not only how computational methods can support therapeutic discovery, but also how scientific progress often comes from understanding why an approach does not work as expected.

More broadly, the project introduced me to scientific thinking, biological complexity, and the interdisciplinary nature of modern quantitative biology. It played a major role in shaping my interest in computational biology and systems-level approaches to research.
